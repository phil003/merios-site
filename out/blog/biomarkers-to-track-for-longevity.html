10 Biomarkers You Should Track for Longevity (And What They Actually Mean)

You've probably heard the phrase "what gets measured gets managed." In healthcare, that's never been more true. While most people focus on the number on the scale, the real indicators of long-term health exist at the molecular level—in your blood.

Biomarkers are measurable biological substances in your body that indicate normal or pathological processes. They're the early warning system for disease, the proof that your lifestyle changes are working, and the data points that separate proactive health management from reactive medicine.

The longevity community has zeroed in on a specific set of biomarkers worth tracking. These aren't vanity metrics—they're scientifically-backed predictors of how long you'll live and, more importantly, how well you'll live.

Let's break down the 10 biomarkers that matter most, what they mean, and what you can actually do about them.

Why Biomarker Tracking Matters for Longevity

Before we dive into specifics, it's worth understanding why biomarkers have become central to preventive health strategies. Traditional medicine waits for symptoms. You don't visit the doctor until something hurts or you feel sick. By then, significant damage may have already occurred.

Biomarkers flip this script. They reveal problems before you feel them. A spike in hsCRP can signal inflammation years before a heart attack. Elevated fasting glucose can indicate metabolic dysfunction long before a diabetes diagnosis. This window of time—sometimes years—is your chance to intervene.

The 10 Essential Longevity Biomarkers

1. HbA1c (Glycated Hemoglobin)

What it measures: Your average blood glucose over the past 2-3 months. It's more reliable than a single fasting glucose test because it captures your whole-day glucose patterns.

Optimal range: Below 5.5% (for non-diabetics; 5.5-5.9% is prediabetic range)

Why it matters: HbA1c is one of the strongest predictors of lifespan. Elevated glucose accelerates aging in nearly every tissue in your body through a process called glycation, where glucose molecules bind to proteins and create inflammatory compounds. Even if you're not diabetic, an HbA1c in the 5.7-6.0% range is associated with increased cardiovascular risk and cognitive decline.

How to improve it:

  • Reduce refined carbohydrate intake and prioritize low glycemic index foods
  • Eat protein and healthy fat with every meal to slow glucose absorption
  • Time-restrict eating to a consistent 8-10 hour window
  • Move for 10 minutes after meals to improve glucose clearance
  • Prioritize sleep; poor sleep disrupts glucose metabolism

2. hsCRP (High-Sensitivity C-Reactive Protein)

What it measures: Inflammation in your bloodstream. Regular CRP tests measure gross inflammation; high-sensitivity CRP detects sub-clinical inflammation you wouldn't otherwise notice.

Optimal range: Below 1.0 mg/L (below 0.5 is ideal)

Why it matters: Chronic low-grade inflammation is the silent driver of aging. It accelerates cardiovascular disease, neurodegenerative disease, cancer, and metabolic disease. An elevated hsCRP often exists long before symptoms, making it a critical early warning system.

How to improve it:

  • Eliminate processed foods and seed oils (high in inflammatory omega-6)
  • Increase anti-inflammatory foods: fatty fish, leafy greens, berries, turmeric
  • Regular exercise (even 30 minutes of walking daily reduces inflammation)
  • Improve sleep quality and duration
  • Manage stress; chronic stress raises CRP
  • Consider intermittent fasting (12-16 hour fasts have shown anti-inflammatory effects)

3. Vitamin D

What it measures: Circulating 25-hydroxyvitamin D, the storage form of vitamin D in your body.

Optimal range: 40-60 ng/mL (some researchers argue for 50-80 ng/mL for longevity)

Why it matters: Vitamin D is less of a vitamin and more of a hormone that regulates over 200 genes. Deficiency is linked to increased mortality from all causes, weakened immunity, higher cancer risk, and accelerated cognitive decline. The majority of Americans are insufficient or deficient.

How to improve it:

  • Get 20-30 minutes of midday sun exposure several times per week (season and latitude dependent)
  • Eat vitamin D-rich foods: fatty fish, egg yolks, mushrooms exposed to sunlight
  • Consider supplementation (1000-4000 IU daily, or higher if deficient; test to confirm)
  • Combine with K2 and calcium for optimal bone health

4. Ferritin

What it measures: Your body's iron storage. It's an indirect measure of total body iron and, at high levels, indicates oxidative stress.

Optimal range: 30-100 ng/mL for men; 20-80 ng/mL for women

Why it matters: High ferritin accelerates aging. Iron generates reactive oxygen species (free radicals) that damage DNA and proteins. People with elevated ferritin have higher rates of heart disease and cancer. Conversely, very low ferritin reduces oxygen delivery and energy production.

How to improve it:

  • If high: Reduce red meat intake, donate blood, increase antioxidants (berries, dark chocolate)
  • If low: Increase iron-rich foods (red meat, spinach), eat with vitamin C (citrus, peppers)
  • Avoid iron supplements unless deficient
  • Monitor through regular testing

5. TSH (Thyroid-Stimulating Hormone)

What it measures: Your pituitary's signal to your thyroid to produce thyroid hormones. It's the primary test for thyroid function.

Optimal range: 0.5-2.5 mIU/L (some functional medicine doctors prefer 1.0-2.0)

Why it matters: Your thyroid controls metabolism, energy, body temperature, and mood. Even subclinical hypothyroidism (TSH in the 3-5 range) is associated with weight gain, fatigue, depression, and cardiovascular risk. Thyroid function declines with age; monitoring catches it early.

How to improve it:

  • If hypothyroid: Get adequate selenium, zinc, and iodine (seafood, seaweed)
  • Manage stress (cortisol suppresses thyroid function)
  • Improve sleep quality
  • Address nutrient deficiencies; B vitamins and iron support thyroid health
  • If TSH is elevated, consult an endocrinologist

6. LDL-P or ApoB (LDL Particle Count or Apolipoprotein B)

What it measures: The number of LDL cholesterol particles in your bloodstream (not the amount of cholesterol). This is far more predictive than total cholesterol or even LDL-C.

Optimal range: LDL-P below 1000 nmol/L; ApoB below 70 mg/dL

Why it matters: You can have "normal" LDL cholesterol and still have a high particle count—meaning more atherogenic particles damaging your arteries. This is a major blind spot in conventional medicine. ApoB is increasingly the preferred marker; it's a single test that captures all atherogenic particles.

How to improve it:

  • Reduce refined carbohydrates and added sugars
  • Increase soluble fiber (oats, beans, flaxseeds)
  • Add plant sterols and stanols to diet (found in some fortified foods)
  • Exercise regularly (strength and aerobic training both help)
  • Don't fear healthy fats; focus on particle count, not fat quantity

7. Testosterone / Estradiol

What it measures: Sex hormone levels, which decline with age but shouldn't decline as much as they typically do.

Optimal range (men): Total testosterone 600-900 ng/dL; Free testosterone 9-30 pg/mL Optimal range (women): Estradiol 30-400 pg/mL (cycle dependent); free testosterone 0.5-4.5 pg/mL

Why it matters: Testosterone and estradiol regulate muscle mass, bone density, mental health, sexual function, and metabolic health. Decline in these hormones accelerates aging and increases disease risk. Both men and women need both hormones.

How to improve it:

  • Strength training (builds muscle and stimulates hormone production)
  • Adequate protein intake (essential for hormone synthesis)
  • Manage weight; excess body fat increases estrogen dysregulation
  • Sleep 7-9 hours nightly (hormones are produced during sleep)
  • Reduce endocrine-disrupting chemicals (plastics, pesticides)
  • Manage cortisol; chronic stress suppresses sex hormones

8. DHEA-S (Dehydroepiandrosterone Sulfate)

What it measures: Your body's most abundant steroid hormone, a precursor to testosterone and estrogen.

Optimal range: 200-500 μg/dL (varies by age and sex; declines with age)

Why it matters: DHEA-S is a remarkable anti-aging molecule. It supports muscle, bone, cognitive function, and immune health. People with higher DHEA-S live longer. Unlike testosterone, DHEA-S doesn't require prescription supplementation in most cases—it responds well to lifestyle.

How to improve it:

  • Regular intense exercise (especially strength training and HIIT)
  • Adequate sleep; DHEA-S is produced during sleep
  • Manage stress; chronic stress depletes DHEA-S
  • Nutrient sufficiency; B vitamins support production
  • Supplement with pregnenolone or DHEA if genuinely deficient (requires testing and medical guidance)

9. Homocysteine

What it measures: An amino acid byproduct of methionine metabolism. Elevated levels are an independent cardiovascular risk factor.

Optimal range: Below 10 μmol/L (below 8 is ideal)

Why it matters: High homocysteine damages blood vessel walls and promotes atherosclerosis. It's a predictor of heart attack and stroke that's often overlooked. Interestingly, homocysteine is very responsive to B vitamins, making it one of the easiest biomarkers to improve.

How to improve it:

  • B6, B12, and folate directly lower homocysteine
  • Eat leafy greens, beans, and whole grains (folate sources)
  • Include animal products (B12 source) or supplement if vegan
  • Get enough B6 (chickpeas, fish, potatoes)
  • Reduce coffee and alcohol (both elevate homocysteine)
  • Manage stress and sleep

10. Omega-3 Index

What it measures: The percentage of omega-3 fatty acids (EPA and DHA) in your red blood cells' cell membrane.

Optimal range: Above 8% (the higher, the better; below 4% is risky)

Why it matters: The Omega-3 Index predicts sudden cardiac death as well as traditional risk factors like cholesterol. Omega-3s reduce inflammation, support brain health, improve mood, and protect your cardiovascular system. Most people are deficient.

How to improve it:

  • Eat fatty fish 2-3 times per week (salmon, sardines, mackerel, herring)
  • Take a high-quality fish oil or algae supplement (aim for 1000-2000mg combined EPA+DHA daily)
  • Reduce omega-6 intake (seed oils, processed foods) to balance the ratio
  • Flaxseeds and walnuts contain ALA (converts to EPA/DHA, but conversion is inefficient)
  • Retest in 3-4 months to confirm supplementation is working

Making Sense of Your Biomarkers

Numbers are only useful if they tell a story. One high marker means something. The same marker in the context of five others means something different. Elevated inflammation with high glucose and low vitamin D tells a different story than inflammation from a recent infection.

If you haven't had comprehensive blood work done, now's the time. Request a lipid panel, metabolic panel, inflammation markers, thyroid panel, sex hormones, DHEA-S, homocysteine, and vitamin D. Most of these aren't standard on routine checkups, so you may need to ask specifically or work with a functional medicine practitioner.
