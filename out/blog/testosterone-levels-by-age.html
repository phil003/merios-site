Testosterone Levels by Age: What's Normal, Optimal, and When to Worry

Testosterone is often called the "male hormone," but this framing misses the fuller picture. Yes, men have far more of it than women, but testosterone is crucial for both sexes: bone density, muscle mass, mood, cognitive function, sexual function, and energy levels all depend on adequate testosterone.

The challenge: testosterone naturally declines with age, and the line between "normal," "low," and "optimal" is blurry. Lab reference ranges don't distinguish between what's medically normal and what supports actual health and vitality.

Testosterone Reference Ranges by Age (Men)

Most labs provide a single reference range for total testosterone: approximately 264-916 ng/dL (or 9.2-31.8 nmol/L in SI units). This range is derived from population averages, not from what research suggests is optimal for health.

A more nuanced picture emerges when you stratify by age:

Age 20-29

  • Average total testosterone: 400-700 ng/dL
  • Range (5th-95th percentile): 350-800 ng/dL
  • Optimal for muscle/strength/sexual function: 600-800 ng/dL

A 20-year-old with testosterone at 264 ng/dL is technically "normal" by lab standards but likely experiencing low testosterone symptoms.

Age 30-39

  • Average total testosterone: 350-650 ng/dL
  • Range (5th-95th percentile): 300-750 ng/dL
  • Optimal range: 550-750 ng/dL

This is where the first noticeable decline appears for many men. The decrease is gradual—roughly 1% annually after age 30, though significant individual variation exists.

Age 40-49

  • Average total testosterone: 300-600 ng/dL
  • Range (5th-95th percentile): 250-700 ng/dL
  • Optimal range: 500-700 ng/dL

By the 40s, a natural decline is evident, yet many men still maintain robust testosterone levels. This is where lifestyle factors—sleep, exercise, body composition, stress—become increasingly important.

Age 50-59

  • Average total testosterone: 250-550 ng/dL
  • Range (5th-95th percentile): 200-650 ng/dL
  • Optimal range: 450-650 ng/dL

The prevalence of low testosterone (below 300 ng/dL) rises notably in this decade, affecting approximately 15-20% of men.

Age 60-69

  • Average total testosterone: 200-500 ng/dL
  • Range (5th-95th percentile): 150-600 ng/dL
  • Optimal range: 400-600 ng/dL

By 60, about 25-30% of men have testosterone below 300 ng/dL. However, many maintain healthy levels into their 70s and beyond.

Age 70+

  • Average total testosterone: 150-450 ng/dL
  • Optimal range: 350-550 ng/dL

The incidence of low testosterone continues rising with age, though causation is complex—obesity, metabolic disease, sleep apnea, and medications contribute significantly to age-related decline.

The Important Distinction: Total vs. Free Testosterone

The testosterone measured in standard blood tests is total testosterone—the bulk of which is bound to sex hormone-binding globulin (SHBG) or albumin. Only about 2-3% is "free" testosterone, the unbound fraction that actually enters cells and exerts effects.

Here's the complication: SHBG levels vary substantially between individuals based on genetics, age, body fat percentage, liver health, and thyroid function.

Two men with identical total testosterone of 450 ng/dL may have completely different free testosterone levels:

  • Man A: SHBG 24 nmol/L → Free testosterone ~8 ng/dL (may have symptoms)
  • Man B: SHBG 45 nmol/L → Free testosterone ~5 ng/dL (more likely to have symptoms)

Key takeaway: If total testosterone is in the low-normal range (250-400 ng/dL), it's worth checking free testosterone or calculating bioavailable testosterone. Many men with symptoms of low testosterone have low-normal total testosterone but clearly low free testosterone.

Reference ranges for free testosterone:

  • Young adult men: 5-21 ng/dL
  • Optimal range: 10-21 ng/dL
  • Below 5 ng/dL: Usually symptomatic

Symptoms of Low Testosterone

Not all testosterone decline causes problems. Some men maintain full function with levels in the 300s. Others develop symptoms in the 400s. This is partly genetic variation and partly other health factors (sleep quality, cardiovascular fitness, insulin sensitivity).

Common symptoms include:

  • Sexual: Reduced libido, erectile dysfunction, reduced ejaculation volume
  • Physical: Decreased muscle mass and strength (despite consistent training), increased fat deposition (especially abdominal), loss of body hair, reduced bone density
  • Cognitive: Brain fog, difficulty concentrating, reduced mental clarity
  • Mood: Depression, anxiety, irritability, reduced motivation
  • Energy: Persistent fatigue, reduced exercise tolerance
  • Metabolic: Insulin resistance, metabolic syndrome, increased cardiovascular risk

Important: These symptoms are nonspecific. Depression, sleep apnea, thyroid dysfunction, and chronic stress can produce identical symptom clusters. A single low testosterone test doesn't prove causation.

When and How to Get Tested

Timing Matters

Testosterone varies throughout the day (circadian rhythm) with highest levels in early morning, declining through the day. It's also influenced by:

  • Stress: Acute stress acutely suppresses testosterone; chronic stress impairs recovery
  • Sleep: Poor sleep substantially lowers testosterone (one night of poor sleep can drop testosterone 10-15%)
  • Illness: Acute infection or inflammation temporarily lowers testosterone

Optimal testing protocol:

  • Fasting morning test (8-10 AM)
  • After at least 7 hours sleep
  • When you haven't been acutely stressed or ill
  • If abnormal, repeat in 2-4 weeks before making treatment decisions

Baseline Testing Recommendations

The Endocrine Society (the authoritative body on hormone guidelines) recommends testosterone testing for men with:

  • Sexual dysfunction
  • Infertility
  • Decreased libido and/or erectile dysfunction
  • Symptoms consistent with low testosterone
  • Opioid or glucocorticoid use (suppresses testosterone)
  • Conditions associated with low testosterone (HIV, type 2 diabetes, obesity)

If you're asymptomatic, testing isn't routinely recommended until age 65-70. However, if you're interested in optimizing health and have access to testing, baseline measurement at 30-40 can be useful for tracking changes over time.

Beyond Total Testosterone

Comprehensive assessment includes:

  • Total testosterone: Primary measurement
  • Free testosterone or calculated bioavailable testosterone: Important if total is low-normal
  • SHBG: Explains variation between individuals
  • LH/FSH: Assesses whether low testosterone is from testicular dysfunction vs. pituitary dysfunction
  • Estradiol: Some men convert excess testosterone to estrogen; elevated estradiol can paradoxically worsen symptoms
  • Prolactin: Suppresses testosterone if elevated

Testosterone in Women

Women produce 10-15 times less testosterone than men but it's still essential for:

  • Bone density
  • Muscle mass
  • Libido and sexual satisfaction
  • Mood and motivation
  • Metabolic health

Reference ranges for women:

  • Premenopausal: 15-70 ng/dL (total testosterone)
  • Free testosterone: 0.5-4.3 pg/mL

Testosterone declines with age and drops more sharply after menopause. Women with testosterone below 20 ng/dL often experience reduced libido and mood symptoms. However, very high testosterone in women (&gt;70 ng/dL) can indicate polycystic ovary syndrome (PCOS) and should be investigated.

Lifestyle Factors That Influence Testosterone

If your testosterone is low-normal or declining, these factors can meaningfully influence levels:

Sleep Quality and Duration

Sleep deprivation (even partial: 5-6 hours nightly) reduces testosterone by 10-15%. One night of poor sleep can drop testosterone 20-30%.

  • Target: 7-9 hours nightly
  • Prioritize sleep consistency (consistent bedtime/wake time)
  • Address sleep disorders (sleep apnea in particular suppresses testosterone)
  • Testosterone recovers within days of improved sleep

Exercise

Resistance training is particularly effective:

  • Heavy strength training increases testosterone acutely (during workout and for hours after)
  • Long-term training increases baseline testosterone
  • Endurance exercise at very high intensity can slightly suppress testosterone, but moderate cardio has no negative effect
  • Sedentary lifestyle is strongly associated with low testosterone

Effective approach: 2-4 sessions weekly of resistance training (8-12 repetitions, moderate-heavy load).

Body Composition

Excess body fat, particularly visceral fat (abdominal), is strongly associated with low testosterone:

  • Each 10 kg of excess body fat is associated with approximately 20-30 ng/dL reduction in testosterone
  • Elevated aromatase activity in fat converts testosterone to estrogen, further suppressing testosterone
  • Weight loss of 5-10% body weight can increase testosterone by 50-100 ng/dL

Metabolic Health

Insulin resistance suppresses testosterone:

  • Prediabetes and type 2 diabetes associated with 15-25% lower testosterone
  • Metabolic syndrome (elevated triglycerides, low HDL, abdominal obesity, hypertension) strongly associated with low testosterone
  • Improving insulin sensitivity through diet and exercise increases testosterone

Nutritional Factors

Zinc: Critical cofactor for testosterone production. Deficiency suppresses testosterone; supplementation may increase testosterone in deficient individuals.

  • Target: 8-11 mg daily for men
  • Sources: Oysters, beef, chicken, pumpkin seeds

Vitamin D: Associated with testosterone levels; observational studies show correlation.

  • Target: 1000-4000 IU daily (based on blood levels)
  • Some studies suggest maintaining 25(OH)D above 30 ng/mL supports testosterone

Overall caloric balance: Severe caloric restriction suppresses testosterone. Moderate energy deficit for weight loss has minimal effect if protein intake is maintained.

Stress Management

Chronic elevated cortisol suppresses testosterone:

  • Stress reduction techniques (meditation, yoga, breathing exercises) can support testosterone
  • This effect is modest compared to sleep and exercise but meaningful over time

Alcohol and Substance Use

  • Alcohol excess suppresses testosterone acutely and chronically
  • Cannabis use associated with lower testosterone
  • Avoid patterns that disrupt sleep or increase stress

The Testosterone Treatment Controversy

Here's what I'm not going to tell you: whether you should or shouldn't pursue testosterone replacement therapy (TRT). That's a medical decision requiring a qualified endocrinologist, not an internet article.

What the evidence shows:

Potential benefits of TRT:

  • Increases muscle mass and strength when combined with resistance training
  • Improves sexual function in men with low testosterone
  • Improves mood and energy in men with symptoms
  • Increases bone density, reducing fracture risk in men with low bone mass

Known risks and unknowns:

  • Increases hematocrit (red blood cell concentration); requires monitoring
  • May slightly increase cardiovascular risk (evidence conflicting and evolving)
  • Suppresses fertility (reversible but takes months to recover)
  • Can elevate estradiol if not monitored
  • Requires ongoing monitoring and is never "set and forget"

If you have symptoms of low testosterone and are considering treatment, consult an endocrinologist or physician experienced in hormone management. They'll assess whether your testosterone is actually low, whether your symptoms correlate with testosterone status, and whether treatment is appropriate for your individual situation.

Bottom Line

Testosterone naturally declines with age—approximately 1% annually after 30. Whether this decline becomes problematic depends on:

  • Your baseline testosterone
  • Your symptom load
  • Your modifiable lifestyle factors (sleep, exercise, body composition, stress)
  • Your individual genetic variation in hormone sensitivity

Before considering medical intervention, optimizing modifiable factors—particularly sleep and exercise—can meaningfully support testosterone. If you have symptoms and are curious about your levels, a single fasting morning test is inexpensive and informative. And if your testosterone is truly low and symptoms are significant, working with a hormone-knowledgeable provider can help determine whether treatment is appropriate for you.
