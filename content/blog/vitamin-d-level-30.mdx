## Is Vitamin D 30 ng/mL Normal?

**Vitamin D 30 ng/mL is technically "normal" by lab ranges, but functionally suboptimal.** Most labs list 30–100 as "normal," but modern research and integrative doctors target 40–60 as optimal. A level of 30 puts you at the lower end of acceptable but won't prevent immediate harm.

## Where Does 30 ng/mL Fall?

| Category | Range (ng/mL) |
|----------|----------------|
| Deficient | &lt;20 |
| Insufficient | 20–29 |
| Normal (lab range) | 30–100 |
| **Your value (30)** | **Just above deficiency** |
| Optimal (functional) | 40–60 |
| Excessive (rare) | &gt;150 |

### What This Means

At 30, you're not deficient, and you won't have acute symptoms like bone pain or muscle weakness. But you're missing the protective benefits of higher vitamin D levels — stronger bones, better immune function, improved mood, and lower inflammation.

## Why "Normal" Isn't Always Optimal

Vitamin D lab ranges were historically set based on the level that *prevents rickets* (a severe bone disease). That threshold is around 20. But modern research shows that levels of 40–60 offer far better protection for:

- **Bone health:** Higher vitamin D correlates with better bone density and lower fracture risk.
- **Immune function:** Vitamin D activates immune cells; levels below 40 are linked to higher infection and autoimmune rates.
- **Mood and mental health:** Deficiency is associated with depression and seasonal affective disorder.
- **Inflammation:** Optimal vitamin D suppresses chronic inflammation markers.

At 30, you're protecting yourself from the worst outcomes, but you're not getting the full benefit.

## Risk Factors for Low Vitamin D

You might have 30 because of:

- **Limited sun exposure:** If you live in northern climates, work indoors, or avoid sun (wise for skin cancer prevention, but vitamin D does suffer).
- **Dark skin:** Melanin reduces vitamin D synthesis. People with darker skin tones need 3–6 times more sun exposure or supplementation to reach the same levels.
- **Malabsorption disorders:** Celiac disease, Crohn's disease, and IBS reduce vitamin D absorption.
- **Older age:** Skin becomes less efficient at vitamin D synthesis after age 50.
- **Dietary gaps:** Fatty fish, egg yolks, and fortified dairy are vitamin D sources; vegan diets often fall short without supplementation.

## What to Do Next

1. **Start supplementation:** 2,000–4,000 IU of vitamin D3 daily is safe and effective for most adults. Take it with a fat-containing meal for better absorption.

2. **Retest in 12 weeks:** After 12 weeks of supplementation, get your level rechecked. Aim for 40–60 ng/mL. If you're moving slowly, your doctor might suggest a higher dose (up to 6,000 IU daily is safe).

3. **Increase sun exposure (safely):** 10–30 minutes of midday sun several times a week supports vitamin D synthesis — without overdoing it. Sunscreen blocks UV-B, so don't rely on it alone for vitamin D; supplement regardless.

4. **Eat vitamin D-rich foods:** Salmon, mackerel, sardines, egg yolks, and fortified milk and plant-based milks all help. But it's hard to reach optimal levels from food alone; supplements are usually necessary.
